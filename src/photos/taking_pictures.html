Depth of Field, Movement, Grainy

Aperture or F number
The opening in the lens, the amount of light that enters the camera implies: Depth of Field: How many planes are in focus.
  • Smaller f-number 1.8 More Light Fewer planes in focus, allows faster shutter speeds
  • Larger f-number 22 Less Light More planes in focus, requires slower shutter speeds

Shutter Speed or Speed
Determines how long the camera's sensor is exposed to light. implies Freeze/blur subject or camera movement
  • Faster 1000 freezes motion, but requires more light
  • Slower 60 causes speed blur, or blurs if camera is not held steady, requires less light
ISO or ASA
Sensitivity of the camera's sensor to light implies How much light is needed
  • Lower 64 Greater quality, less pixelated or grainy, needs more light
  • Higher 800 Lower quality, more pixelated or grainy, needs less light

Faces

      Face Shapes:

    Round: Shooting slightly from above helps slim the face and define the jawline. Avoid shooting from below, as it can emphasize roundness.

    Square: Shooting at eye level or slightly from below softens the angles of the jawline. Avoid shooting from high above, which can accentuate the square shape.

    Oval: Oval faces are generally considered versatile and can be flattering from various angles. Experiment to find what works best for your subject.

    Heart: Shooting slightly from above or at eye level can balance the wider forehead with the narrower chin. Avoid shooting from below, which can make the forehead appear even wider.

    Long/Rectangular: Shooting at eye level or slightly from below can help shorten the face. Avoid shooting from above, as it can elongate the face even more.

Facial Features:

    Forehead: A high forehead can be minimized by shooting slightly from below. A low forehead can be balanced by shooting from above.

    Nose: A long nose can appear shorter when photographed straight on or from slightly above. A wide nose can be narrowed by shooting from slightly below.

    Chin: A prominent chin can be softened by shooting slightly from above. A weak chin can be strengthened by shooting from slightly below.

Tips
      Eyes:  Always focus on the eyes, focal point of most portraits.
      Lighting:  Soft, diffused light is generally flattering for portraits, while harsh light can create unflattering shadows.

  1. A-DEP Automatic Depth of Field AE: This is the final mode in the Creative Zone, and is meant for taking pictures of large groups of people, landscapes with foreground detail, or any subject with elements of interest at varying distances from the camera. This mode puts the camera in control of both the shutter speed and aperture values, but you can adjust the other exposure variables. (This mode cannot be used if the lens focus mode is set to manual.) When shooting in Automatic Depth of Field AE, the camera sets both the lens aperture and focus distance to achieve a sharp focus over a wide depth of field. It uses the autofocus system to measure the distance to the subjects covered by each of the nine autofocus zones, and then attempts to set the focusing distance and lens aperture so as to render all subject areas in sharp focus. (This is a really useful mode, I wish other manufacturers would steal a page from Canon's playbook on this one.)
  2. C1, C2, Camera User Settings Modes: These three final settings on the Mode dial allow you to save two individual sets of camera settings for quick recall. Useful when shooting an event with multiple light sources, such as a wedding and reception, these custom modes let you register camera settings for specific conditions and easily go between modes. You can save most of the camera's shooting mode options as well as the Custom Function settings. Settings are registered as C1 or C2 through the Camera User Setting menu.

1. Hyperfocal Distance (H):

H = (f^2) / (N * c)

Where:

    f = Focal length of the lens. in mm 24mm, 50mm, etc.
    N = Aperture f-number
    c = Circle of confusion (a measure of acceptable blur)
		Example numbers
			Full-frame: 0.025mm to 0.030mm
			APS-C: 0.015mm to 0.020mm
			Micro Four Thirds: 0.010mm to 0.015mm
			Medium Format: 0.040mm to 0.050mm
		Sensor resolution:
			Higher resolution sensors generally require smaller CoC values for optimal sharpness.
			Viewing distance: Photos viewed at a closer distance will require a smaller CoC to appear sharp.
			Print size: Larger prints will require a smaller CoC to maintain sharpness.
			Personal preference: Some photographers prefer a slightly larger CoC for a more forgiving depth of field, while others prefer a smaller CoC for maximum sharpness.

2. Near Focus Limit (Dn):

Dn = (H * D) / (H + (D - f))

Where:

    H = Hyperfocal distance
    D = Distance to the subject
    f = Focal length of the lens

3. Far Focus Limit (Df):

Df = (H * D) / (H - (D - f))

Where:

    H = Hyperfocal distance
    D = Distance to the subject
    f = Focal length of the lens

  class HandleClick {
    - authService: AuthenticationService
    - pageService: PageService
    + process(notification: Notification): void
    - checkUserLoggedIn(): boolean
    - openPage(pageId: String): void
    - showLoginScreen(): void
  }

note bottom of handleClick
  Flow:
  1. User receives and clicks notification
  2. HandleClick.process() is called
  3. If user is logged in, open indicated page
  4. If not logged in, show login screen,
     user logs in, then open indicated page
  5. User interacts with the page
end note
